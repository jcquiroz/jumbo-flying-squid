How the Chile, China & CALAMASUR Models Differ

Why the three assessments disagree

All three groups assessed the same species (Dosidicus gigas), the same region (Southeast Pacific / FAO Area 87), and broadly the same catch data updated to 2024 — including the sharp ~52% drop in landings in 2024. Yet they reached different verdicts on stock status. The divergence comes almost entirely from model architecture and how each model treats the 2024 catch collapse and environmental variability, not from the underlying data. The sections below walk through where the models agree and where they part ways.

Where they fundamentally differ

1. Depletion model vs. pure production model

The single biggest structural difference is CALAMASUR’s two-stage depletion approach. Chile (SPiCT) and China both fit a surplus production model directly to catch and a CPUE abundance index — they infer biomass from the relationship between catch and a relative abundance signal. CALAMASUR instead first fits a generalized depletion model at monthly steps that uses fishing effort (not CPUE) as the mechanistic driver, tracking within-season depletion and recruitment pulses for each fleet; the biomass series it produces then feeds a Pella-Tomlinson model at Stage 2.

This matters because production models assume the CPUE index is proportional (or a known function) of true abundance — an assumption widely questioned for fast-growing, highly migratory squid. CALAMASUR’s design deliberately avoids relying on standardized CPUE as an abundance index, estimating natural mortality inside the model instead (≈2.0/yr, consistent with the short life cycle).

2. How the environment enters

China and CALAMASUR both build the ENSO cycle into the model, letting productivity parameters switch between El Niño and La Niña regimes — China favoured a model where both carrying capacity K and growth rate r vary (the “Kr” model), while CALAMASUR found best support for only r varying (higher in El Niño: 2.93/yr vs 0.48/yr in La Niña). Chile’s SPiCT run does not encode the environment explicitly; the authors instead flag environmental variability and the three squid phenotypes as caveats to be addressed in future work.

3. What each does with the 2024 catch drop

This is the crux of the differing verdicts:

  • China interprets the healthy stock despite the 2024 landings collapse as evidence of an El Niño effect on population dynamics — the drop reflects environment/availability, not depletion. Verdict: still healthy.
  • CALAMASUR sees a stock in a regime of wide fluctuations with no sustained downward biomass trend and exploitation mostly below 40%; not overfished, but the rising exploitation rate means overfishing may be occurring, and MSY/BMSY are judged inadequate reference points.
  • Chile reads the 52% catch reduction plus a general decline in the abundance indices as a genuine downturn, placing the 2025 stock in the Kobe red zone (in the best-fitting, six-index case). SPiCT trusts the declining CPUE signal more directly.

4. Reference points and management outputs

Chile’s SPiCT produces conventional Kobe-plot status (B/BMSY, F/FMSY) and explicit 2026 TAC advice (267–989 kt across cases; ~467 kt in the best case). China produces Kobe status with terminal-year healthy-status probabilities (58–88%). CALAMASUR argues that under strong fluctuations MSY and BMSY are not valid reference points and a standard Kobe plot cannot be built, calling instead for new environment-aware reference points based on mean latent productivity (still imprecise).

Points of genuine agreement

Despite the different headlines, the three assessments converge on several things: the stock is heavily influenced by the ENSO cycle; biomass fluctuates widely rather than showing a clean trend; exploitation has been rising in recent years; and the standardized CPUE indices need improvement (spatiotemporal structure, multiple phenotypes/sizes). Both China and CALAMASUR independently conclude that environmental cycles drive productivity, and even Chile — while not modelling it — names the three phenotypes and environmental variability as the key unresolved issues.

How to read the disagreement

The spread of verdicts is best understood as a model-structure spectrum rather than a data dispute:

  • The more a model trusts declining CPUE as a direct abundance signal (Chile, SPiCT), the more pessimistic the status.
  • The more a model attributes the 2024 drop to environment/availability (China), the more optimistic the status.
  • The more a model relies on effort-based depletion dynamics and treats MSY as unreliable under fluctuations (CALAMASUR), the more it lands on a cautious middle — “not overfished, watch the exploitation rate.”

No single model is definitively “right”; each encodes a different, defensible hypothesis about why catches fell in 2024. That is precisely why the SPRFMO Squid Working Group runs them in parallel.
