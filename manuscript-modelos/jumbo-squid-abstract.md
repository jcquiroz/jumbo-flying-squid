# Abstract

**Reconciling competing stock assessments for jumbo flying squid (*Dosidicus gigas*) in the South-East Pacific through probabilistic multi-model risk analysis**

The jumbo flying squid (*Dosidicus gigas*) supports the world's largest invertebrate fishery, with annual landings exceeding one million tonnes in the South-East Pacific. The species' short, semelparous life cycle and pronounced sensitivity to the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) pose persistent challenges for stock assessment and management under the South Pacific Regional Fisheries Management Organisation (SPRFMO). At the 2025 SPRFMO Scientific Committee (SC13), three parties presented assessments for the same stock, using broadly overlapping catch data updated to 2024, yet reaching markedly different conclusions about status. We synthesised the three assessments—the CALAMASUR two-stage effort-driven depletion plus Pella–Tomlinson production model (1990–2024), the Chilean IFOP SPiCT surplus-production model, and the Chinese Shanghai Ocean University Bayesian state-space model with explicit ENSO regimes—and examined why they diverge. We then adapted the reference-point-based multi-model risk-analysis framework developed for eastern Pacific tropical tunas (Aires-da-Silva et al., 2020; Maunder et al., 2020) to *D. gigas*. The framework organises competing candidate models as weighted hypotheses in a three-level tree, scores each on six discrete metrics (expert judgement, convergence, fit, plausibility of parameters and results, and diagnostics), and combines per-model distributions of reference-point ratios via Bayesian model averaging to yield exceedance probabilities that can directly inform harvest control rules. The three verdicts (healthy in China, not overfished but possibly overfishing in CALAMASUR, Kobe red zone in Chile) align with a model-structure spectrum: the more a model attributes the 2024 catch drop to environment-driven availability, the more optimistic the verdict. We argue that effort- and capacity-based controls are insufficient alone, that MSY-based reference points are unreliable under strong environment-driven fluctuations, and that probabilistic, environment-aware, model-averaged advice is the defensible route forward. The weighted model set assembled here can seed a future Management Strategy Evaluation for the fishery.
